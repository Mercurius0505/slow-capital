# THE 7-DAY BEGINNER TRADING ROADMAP
## Full Lesson Content — Forex, ETFs & Stocks

> **Important framing note:** This roadmap teaches you to develop the *skills* required to responsibly target your first $100 in real profits. It does not promise that outcome. Trading carries substantial risk; ESMA reports that 74-89% of retail CFD traders lose money. The honest goal of week one is: open a demo account, execute your first informed paper trade, and write a personal trading plan. Real capital comes only after that foundation is solid.

---

## DAY 1 — FOUNDATIONS: HOW MARKETS ACTUALLY WORK

### Learning objectives
By the end of today you should be able to: (1) define a financial market in your own words; (2) explain what makes a price move; (3) name the three asset classes this roadmap covers and one defining feature of each.

### 1.1 What a market actually is
A financial market is a coordination mechanism. It exists because two groups of people have opposite needs: *some want to convert money into an asset, others want to convert an asset back into money.* The price you see quoted at any moment is simply the most recent agreement between one buyer and one seller. It is not a fact about the asset's value — it is a snapshot of negotiation.

This distinction matters because beginners often treat the displayed price as if it were objective. It is not. The price reflects current opinion, weighted by capital. When opinion shifts, price shifts.

### 1.2 The three concepts every chart hides
Every quote you see contains three pieces of information that platforms often obscure:

- **Bid** — the highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay.
- **Ask** — the lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept.
- **Spread** — the gap between them. The spread is your immediate, unavoidable transaction cost.

The wider the spread, the less liquid the market. Highly liquid markets like EUR/USD or the S&P 500 ETF have spreads measured in tenths of a cent. Illiquid small-cap stocks can have spreads of several percent. Spreads are the first hidden cost most beginners ignore.

### 1.3 Why prices move
Prices move when the balance of buying pressure and selling pressure tips. Causes include:

1. **New information** — earnings reports, central bank decisions, geopolitical events
2. **Reassessment of existing information** — markets re-weight known facts as conditions change
3. **Liquidity events** — large institutional rebalancing, fund flows, options expiries
4. **Reflexive behavior** — price movements themselves trigger more buying or selling (stop-loss cascades, momentum chasing)

You will *not* be able to predict most of these. The professional response is not prediction but preparation: a plan that performs acceptably across multiple outcomes.

### 1.4 The three asset classes you'll learn
- **Forex** (foreign exchange): the simultaneous buying of one currency and selling of another. Largest market in the world (~$7.5 trillion daily volume). Trades 24 hours, five days a week. Highly leveraged. Most volatile path for beginners.
- **Stocks**: fractional ownership in a company. Returns come from price appreciation and dividends. Time horizon typically months to decades.
- **ETFs** (exchange-traded funds): a single tradeable basket holding many underlying assets. Combines the simplicity of buying one ticker with built-in diversification. Most beginner-appropriate of the three.

### Key terms today
Bid · Ask · Spread · Liquidity · Volume · Volatility

### Common pitfall
Beginners assume "the market" is a single thing. It is not. EUR/USD at 14:00 UTC behaves nothing like a small-cap biotech at the same moment. Rules that apply to one rarely transfer cleanly to another.

Open any chart of EUR/USD on TradingView (free). Identify the bid, ask, and spread. Watch them update for two minutes. Notice that even when "nothing is happening," the spread breathes.

## DAY 2 — CHOOSING YOUR ASSET CLASS

### Learning objectives
Decide which of the three asset classes matches *your* risk tolerance, time availability, and learning style — and understand why this decision matters more than any indicator or strategy you'll ever learn.

### 2.1 Forex — the high-velocity option
Forex pairs trade in tiny units called *pips* (typically 0.0001 of price). Brokers offer leverage up to 30:1 for retail clients in the EU under ESMA rules — meaning a €100 deposit can control €3,000 of currency exposure.

**Why beginners are drawn to it:** low capital requirement, 24-hour access, fast feedback.

**Why most beginners lose money in it:** the same leverage that magnifies gains magnifies losses. A 3% adverse move on 30:1 leverage wipes out the full account. Forex demands strict risk management *before* it demands strategy.

**Suitable if:** you can commit 30+ minutes daily, you handle losses without emotional reaction, and you are willing to spend 3-6 months on demo before risking real capital.

### 2.2 Stocks — the ownership option
Buying a share of Apple makes you a fractional owner of Apple. Your returns come from two sources: capital appreciation (the share price goes up) and dividends (cash distributions).

**Why beginners do well here:** time horizon is forgiving. A diversified stock position held for years tends to perform regardless of timing precision.

**Why beginners struggle:** discipline. Stocks reward patience, but apps are designed to maximize engagement, which incentivizes overtrading.

**Suitable if:** you have a multi-year horizon, you can ignore daily price noise, and your interest is in companies and industries rather than charts.

### 2.3 ETFs — the diversified option
An ETF is a single ticker that holds dozens to thousands of underlying assets. The most common type — a broad index ETF like one tracking the MSCI World — gives you exposure to ~1,500 companies across 23 developed countries with one purchase.

**Why beginners do best here:** diversification is the single most evidence-supported principle in finance. ETFs deliver it in one click. Costs are minimal (~0.07-0.20% annual fees for major index ETFs).

**Why beginners overlook it:** it is "boring." There is no excitement, no daily action, no story to tell at parties.

**Suitable if:** you want long-term wealth building rather than active trading, you can invest monthly via a savings plan (Sparplan), and you measure success in years, not days.

### 2.4 The honest recommendation
For someone starting from zero with the explicit goal of "first $100 in profit responsibly," the order of difficulty (easiest to hardest) is: **ETFs → Stocks → Forex.** Most retail traders who succeed long-term spent their first year primarily in the first two and only added Forex once they had a verified record of disciplined behavior.

## DAY 3 — CHOOSING AND OPENING A BROKER

### Learning objectives
Open a real demo account today, on a regulated broker, with the asset class you chose yesterday.

### 3.1 What "regulated" means and why it is non-negotiable
A regulated broker operates under the supervision of a national financial authority. In the EU, the relevant ones are:
- **BaFin** (Germany)
- **FCA** (UK)
- **CySEC** (Cyprus, common for EU-passporting brokers)
- **AMF** (France)
- **CONSOB** (Italy)

Regulation gives you specific protections: segregated client funds, deposit protection schemes (up to €100,000 in many EU jurisdictions for cash, separate rules for securities), and a complaints process. Unregulated or offshore brokers ("regulated" in St. Vincent or the Marshall Islands) provide effectively none of this.

**Rule:** if a broker's marketing emphasizes high leverage and bonuses, but you have to dig to find their license number, walk away.

### 3.2 EU-friendly brokers worth evaluating
The list below is *not* an endorsement. Each has trade-offs. Verify license numbers on the regulator's website yourself.

| Broker | Strength | Weakness | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Trade Republic** | Lowest fees in DE; ETF Sparpläne free | No proper desktop app; limited tools | ETF & stock beginners in Germany |
| **Scalable Capital** | Strong for ETF savings plans; Prime+ flat fee | Mobile app dominant; less for active trading | Long-term ETF investors |
| **Interactive Brokers** | Professional-grade; broadest market access | Steep learning curve | Serious learners willing to invest time |
| **eToro** | Easy onboarding; copy-trading feature | Higher spreads; copy-trading often misleading | Casual exploration only |
| **DEGIRO** | Low fees; broad European coverage | Customer service inconsistent | Cost-conscious EU investors |

For Forex specifically, regulated CFD brokers in the EU operate under ESMA rules (max 30:1 leverage on majors, negative balance protection). Look for FCA or BaFin licensing.

### 3.3 Why you must use a demo account first
A demo account replicates real market conditions with virtual money. It is the only place to:

- Learn the platform's mechanics without time pressure
- Make every beginner mistake at zero cost
- Build the muscle memory of placing orders correctly
- Test whether your psychology survives even small losses

Demo accounts have one limitation: they do not replicate the *emotional* component of real money. That is fine. You handle psychology in Week 2 onward. This week is purely mechanical.

### 3.4 Account-opening checklist
1. Verify regulator and license number on the regulator's website
2. Read the fee schedule (specifically: spreads, commissions, currency-conversion fees, inactivity fees)
3. Open a demo account first, not a live one
4. Confirm you can view: a price chart, an order ticket, an open positions list, an account history
5. Place a test order: buy and immediately sell one share or 0.01 lot. Verify the trade appears correctly in your history.

## DAY 4 — READING CHARTS

### Learning objectives
Read a price chart with intent: identify the trend, mark support and resistance, and choose the appropriate time frame for your goal.

### 4.1 Why candlesticks
The candlestick chart, developed in 18th-century Japan for the rice market, displays four pieces of information per period: open, high, low, close. A green (or white) candle means the close was above the open — net buying for that period. A red (or black) candle means net selling.

The body of the candle shows the open-to-close range. The wicks show the full high-to-low range. A long upper wick means buyers pushed price up but sellers reclaimed most of the move — a sign of weakness in the up-move.

You do not need to memorize 60 candlestick patterns. You need three: *bullish engulfing*, *bearish engulfing*, and *doji*. The rest are noise.

### 4.2 Trend
A trend is the general direction price is moving over your chosen time frame. Three states exist:

- **Uptrend** — higher highs and higher lows
- **Downtrend** — lower highs and lower lows
- **Range** — neither; price oscillates between two levels

The single most-broken rule by beginners: **do not trade against the trend on your chosen time frame.** Most beginner losses come from "buying the dip" in a downtrend or "shorting the top" in an uptrend.

### 4.3 Support and resistance
**Support** is a price level where buying pressure has historically been strong enough to halt a decline. **Resistance** is the inverse — where selling pressure has historically halted advances.

Support and resistance are not lines; they are zones. Mark them by identifying levels where price has reversed at least twice. The more times a level has been tested, the more significant — until it breaks, at which point old support typically becomes new resistance and vice versa.

### 4.4 Choosing a time frame
Your time frame determines your trade duration:

| Time frame | Trade duration | Suitable for |
|---|---|---|
| 1-minute / 5-minute | Seconds to minutes | Scalping (advanced; not recommended for beginners) |
| 15-minute / 1-hour | Hours | Day trading |
| 4-hour / Daily | Days to weeks | Swing trading (best for beginners) |
| Weekly / Monthly | Months to years | Position investing / ETFs |

Beginners should default to the **daily chart** for analysis and the **4-hour chart** for entry timing. Lower time frames produce more noise per unit of signal.

### Today's action item
Open the daily chart of any stock or ETF you find interesting. Mark two support zones and two resistance zones. Identify whether the current trend is up, down, or sideways. Save the annotated chart.

## DAY 5 — RISK MANAGEMENT (THE MOST IMPORTANT DAY)

### Learning objectives
Internalize one rule, one calculation, and one habit. These three components, properly applied, prevent the catastrophic losses that destroy most beginner accounts.

### 5.1 The 1% rule
**Never risk more than 1% of your account on a single trade.**

This is not advice. It is the operating constraint that separates traders who survive their first year from those who do not. The mathematics are unforgiving: a trader who risks 5% per trade and suffers ten consecutive losses (which happens far more often than beginners expect) loses 40% of their account. A trader who risks 1% loses 9.6%. The first must earn 67% to break even; the second only 11%.

### 5.2 Position sizing — the calculation
Position size is the number of units you buy or sell, derived from three inputs:

1. **Account size** — your total trading capital
2. **Risk per trade** — 1% of account size
3. **Stop-loss distance** — how far in price terms your exit point is from your entry

The formula: **Position size = (Account × Risk %) ÷ Stop-loss distance**

Example: €1,000 account, 1% risk = €10 maximum loss. If your entry is €50 and your stop-loss is €48, your stop distance is €2. Position size = €10 ÷ €2 = 5 shares. If the trade hits your stop, you lose exactly €10 — your predefined maximum.

Use the position-size calculator on the website. Never enter a trade without calculating this first.

### 5.3 Stop-loss and take-profit — the habit
Every trade requires three predefined prices, set *before* you enter:

- **Entry** — the price at which you open the position
- **Stop-loss** — the price at which you exit if wrong
- **Take-profit** — the price at which you exit if right

The ratio between potential profit and potential loss is the **risk-reward ratio**. A trade with a stop-loss of €2 and a take-profit of €6 has a risk-reward of 1:3. Beginners should not take trades with risk-reward below 1:2. This means even with a 50% win rate, you remain profitable.

### 5.4 The psychological function of these rules
The 1% rule, predefined stops, and risk-reward ratios serve a deeper purpose than mathematics: they *remove decision-making in the moment when you are least equipped to make it.* When a trade moves against you, your brain is flooded with cortisol; this is the worst possible time to decide whether to exit. Predefined rules made in calm, before the trade, override panicked decisions made during it.

### Today's action item

## DAY 6 — YOUR FIRST PAPER TRADE

### Learning objectives
Execute one demo trade end to end: thesis, entry, stop, target, and journal entry. Quality over quantity. One well-documented trade today beats ten impulsive ones.

### 6.1 Building a thesis
A thesis is the *reason* you take the trade, written before you take it. If you cannot articulate the thesis in three sentences, you are not ready to enter.

Thesis template:
1. **Setup** — what condition do I observe? *(e.g., "MSCI World ETF has bounced from the €82 zone three times in the past four weeks.")*
2. **Trigger** — what specific event will cause me to enter? *(e.g., "A daily close above €83.50 confirming the bounce.")*
3. **Invalidation** — what condition would prove the thesis wrong? *(e.g., "A daily close below €81.50 means the support has failed.")*

If the trigger fires, you enter. If invalidation fires, you exit. Everything else is noise.

### 6.2 Executing the trade
1. Confirm thesis is in writing
2. Calculate position size using Day 5's formula
3. Place the order with stop-loss and take-profit attached at the same time (most platforms allow this as a "bracket order")
4. Walk away. The hardest part is not watching.

### 6.3 The trade journal
Every trade gets a journal entry, regardless of outcome. The journal is the single highest-leverage tool a developing trader has. After 50 trades, the journal reveals patterns: which setups work, which time of day you trade best, which mistakes you repeat.

Journal template:

```
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Asset: [ticker]
Direction: [long/short]
Thesis: [3 sentences]
Entry: [price]
Stop: [price]
Target: [price]
Position size: [units]
Risk-reward: [ratio]
Outcome: [+/- amount]
Lesson: [one sentence]
```

The most important field is the last one. Every trade must teach you exactly one thing.

### 6.4 Common beginner mistakes (avoid these today)
1. **Moving the stop** when the trade goes against you — the cardinal sin
2. **Closing winners too early** — fear of giving back gains kills risk-reward ratios
3. **Entering without a thesis** — "the chart looks good" is not a thesis
4. **Doubling down on losers** — averaging into a losing position is how accounts die
5. **Trading multiple assets at once on day one** — focus on one trade

### Today's action item
Execute one demo trade following the full process: thesis → calculation → bracket order → journal entry. Save the journal entry as a text file.

## DAY 7 — YOUR REAL-MONEY TRADING PLAN

### Learning objectives
Produce a written trading plan you actually intend to follow. Define the realistic conditions under which you graduate from demo to live, and what your first $100 milestone honestly looks like.

### 7.1 Why a written plan
Unwritten rules are not rules. The act of writing forces specificity, and specificity is the difference between a plan and a wish. A trader without a written plan is making it up in real time, which means decisions will be inconsistent — which guarantees inconsistent results.

### 7.2 Trading plan template

```
PERSONAL TRADING PLAN
=====================

1. PURPOSE
   Why am I trading? (Wealth building / income / learning)
   What is my time horizon? (Years / months / weeks)
   What is success at the 12-month mark?

2. CAPITAL
   Total trading capital: €___
   Maximum loss tolerance for the year: ___% of capital
   Source of funds: (only money I can afford to lose entirely)

3. ASSET CLASS
   Primary: (Forex / Stocks / ETFs)
   Secondary (if any): ___

4. STRATEGY
   Time frame: ___
   Setup type(s): ___
   Specific entry trigger: ___
   Specific exit triggers (stop & target): ___

5. RISK RULES
   Risk per trade: 1% (non-negotiable)
   Maximum simultaneous open trades: ___
   Maximum drawdown before pausing: ___%
   Daily loss limit: ___

6. ROUTINE
   Pre-market preparation: ___ minutes
   Trading hours: ___
   Post-market journaling: ___ minutes
   Weekly review: when?

7. GRADUATION CRITERIA (demo → live)
   I will only fund a live account when:
   - I have completed ___ demo trades
   - My demo win rate is at least 40% with average risk-reward ≥ 1:2
   - I have a journal with one lesson per trade
   - I have followed my plan exactly for ___ consecutive weeks

8. REVIEW SCHEDULE
   Weekly: review journal, identify one improvement
   Monthly: full performance review, plan adjustments
   Quarterly: re-read this document and update if needed
```

### 7.3 The honest reality of the "first $100"
Here is the truth most marketing hides: **earning your first real $100 in profit, sustainably, typically takes 2-6 months of disciplined demo trading followed by careful live trading.** Anyone who promises faster is selling you something.

What is realistic in week one is everything you have just done: opened a demo account, executed a documented trade, written a plan, internalized risk management. These are the conditions under which $100 becomes achievable. They are not the achievement itself.

The traders who succeed long-term treat the first $100 as a *byproduct* of disciplined process, not as a target. The target is the process. The money follows.

### 7.4 What to do next (Week 2 onward)
1. Execute 20 more demo trades over the next four weeks, journaling each one
2. Review your journal weekly. Identify one mistake you keep repeating. Stop doing it.
3. Read one book per month from a quality list: *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator* (Lefèvre), *The Intelligent Investor* (Graham), *Trading in the Zone* (Douglas)
4. Avoid: paid signals, "guru" courses, copy-trading services, anything promising guaranteed returns
5. When your demo metrics meet your graduation criteria, fund a live account with capital you can afford to lose entirely. Start with the smallest possible position size.

### Today's action item
Complete the trading plan template above. Save it as a text file. Re-read it once per week for the next month.
